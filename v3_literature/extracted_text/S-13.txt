Wolfram BODE and Robert HUBER

The substrate-like ‘canonical’ inhibition by the ‘small’ serine proteinase inhibitors and the product- 
like inhibition by the carboxypeptidase inhibitor have provided the only atomic models of protein 
inhibitor - proteinase interactions for about 15 years. The recently published structures of cystatin/ 
stefin - papain complexes and of hirudin - thrombin complexes reveal novel non-substrate-like inter- 
actions, In addition, the structure of pro-carboxypeptidase showes a mode of inactivation which bears 
resemblance to proteinase/protein inhibitor systems. Considerable progress in understanding the 
transition between native and cleaved states of the serpins has also been made by several recent 
structural studies. 
Proteinase inhibitors are important tools of nature for 
regulating the proteolytic activity of their target proteinases, 
for blocking these in emergency cases, or for signaling receptor 
interactions or clearance. Endogenous inhibitors appear to be 
always proteins; small non-proteinaceous inhibitors which 
impair the proteolytic activity of host proteinases are pro- 
duced in microorganisms. 
The number of proteinaceous proteinase inhibitors iso- 
lated and identified so far is extremely large. In a now classical 
review paper, Laskowski and Kato [2] introduced for the 
first time a rational nomenclature by grouping these diverse 
inhibitors into distinct protein families. In the meantime, this 
list of families has considerably expanded with the advent of 
many new inhibitor species, and is still growing. 
The majority of protein inhibitors known and charac- 
terized so far are directed towards serine proteinases. Within 
the last few years, a large number of protein inhibitors of 
cysteine proteinases have also been discovered and charac- 
terized [3,4]. In contrast, only a few protein inhibitors directed 
towards metallo-proteinases (TIMP and PCI, see [5, 61) or 
aspartyl proteinases (see [7-9]) are known to date. The az- 
macroglobulin family presents an exception, as these proteins 
can inhibit all of these proteinases according to a ‘molecular 
trap’ mechanism by virtue of a promiscuous ‘bait region’ (see 
[IOI). 
Until recently, X-ray crystal structures of only a few serine 
proteinase inhibitors, one carboxypeptidase inhibitor, and 
some of their complexes with cognate proteinases were avail- 
able. The X-ray crystal structures of protein inhibitors 
published up to 1985 have been reviewed by Read and James 
[I I]. Since 1986, several more proteinase-inhibitor-related

In this review, we shall attempt to illuminate the character- 
istic structural properties conferring inhibitory activity to pro- 
teins. Nature has used diverse approaches to achieve protein- 
ase inhibition. This is particularly well illustrated by some 
more recently published structures. In a recent minireview 
[24], we have surveyed and evaluated the most recent protein 
inhibitor structures. We would like in this review article to 
extend this survey to all protein inhibitors of proteinases and 
their proteinase complexes for which the spatial atomic struc- 
ture has been determined. These structures are summarized 
(following 11 I]) in Table 1 together with some characteristic 
parameters. In addition, a gallery of representative structural 
models will help the reader to note some characteristic features 
of these structures. 
Protein inhibitors of serine proteinases 
The protein inhibitors directed against serine proteinases 
can be grouped into at least 16 different families based on 
sequence similarity, topological similarity, and mechanism of 
binding [2]. For 11 of these, at least one (and often several) 
representative spatial atomic structure is known to date (see 
Table 1); for some others, such as an Escherichia coli trypsin 
inhibitor [79] and an Ascartr trypsin inhibitor [go], structure 
analyses are underway.

Most of these serine-proteinase-directed inhibitors react 
with cognate enzymes according to a common, substrate- 
like standard mechanism [2, 811. This group of ‘canonical’ 
inhibitors comprises relatively small proteins (or protein do- 
mains of multiheaded inhibitors) of between 29 and around 
190 amino acid residues. They all possess an exposed binding 
loop of a characteristic canonical conformation, but are other- 
wcse unrelated in structure. 
The serpins (serine proteinase inhibitors) [82] form a family 
of homologous, large (glyco-) proteins comprising about 400 
amino acid residues 183,841. Like the canonical inhibitors, the 
serpin inhibitors seem to interact via an exposed binding loop 
with their cognate proteinases; the resulting complexes are, 
however, only transient and collapse with the liberation of a 
cleaved form of different structure and stability. Most serpins 
exhibit inhibitory activity exclusively towards serine protein- 
ases; only a few (such as ovalbumin) apparently lack inhibi- 
tory properties and may have other functions. 
Hirudin binds according to a very different mechanism 
[16- 181. This non-canonical interaction may be used more 
frequently in serine proteinase inhibition, but has so far been 
defined for hirudin only.

The exposed (although often remarkably hydrophobic, 
see in particular [13]) binding loop is further stabilized by 
additional interactions between residues flanking the reactive 
site and the inhibitor core (see also Fig. 9A). In most canonical 
small inhibitors, the P2 residue (BPTI and MPI [13], see Figs 1 
and 5) or the P3 residue (in Kazal, SSI, Bowman-Birk, squash 
seeds and potato inhibitors 2, see Figs 3,4 and 7) is disulfide- 
connected to the hydrophobic core. In addition, side-chain 
spacers and inter-main-chain hydrogen bonds clamp the loop 
to the main body. In some inhibitors (potato inhibitors 1, STI, 
see Figs 2 and 6), the binding loop is instead stabilized by an 
elaborate electrostatic/hydrogen bond network through side 
chains extending from the core to the binding loop (particu- 
larly well illustrated by the two parallel arginine side chains 
of Arg51 and Arg53 of eglin c [55, 581, see Fig. 2). 
Scaffold and binding loop of the free inhibitors are not 
independent units. Amino acid residue replacements in the 
binding loop have in general only small effects on the thermal 
stability (due to their mainly exposed nature). On the other 
hand, replacement of conserved spacer residues, in particular 
those involved in (non)-covalent interactions between the loop 
and the scaffold (see e. g. eglin and OMTKY3, Figs 2 and 7), 
can result in considerable enhancement of the loop mobility 
[91] and reduced rigidity of the scaffold [92 - 941. 
Such an increase in mobility is also observed upon proteo- 
lytic cleavage at the reactive-site 18- 19 bond of ovomucoid 
(Kazal-type) inhibitors: the residues adjacent to the cleaved 
bond become partially disordered with some intra-loop and 
loop-scaffold hydrogen bonds present in the uncleaved (vir- 
gin) inhibitors disrupted, while the inhibitor scaffold essen- 
tially maintains its conformation [45, 951. The reactive-site- 
cleaved inhibitors of ovomucoid third domain and STI de-

Serpins 
Atomic-resolution crystal structures of four specifically 
modified serpin inhibitors (two a,-proteinase inhibitor species 
169, 701, a,-antichymotrypsin [71] and HLEI [72]) and of two 
ovalbumin (non-inhibitory serpin) species (plakalbumin [73] 
and native ovalbumin [74]) are available to date (see Fig. 10). 
In the first four cleaved serpin species, the newly formed 
terminal segments S4A and S1C (see Fig. 10A) are incorpo- 
rated into two different P-pleated sheets A and C; the P1 and 
P1’ residues are separated by a distance of 7 nm (Fig. 1OC). 
The compact and well hydrogen-bonded structure is in agree- 
ment with the higher thermodynamic stability of the cleaved 
species compared with native inhibitors [118, 119, 1221. In the 
two ovalbumin species, the peptide segment equivalent to the 
carboxy-terminal strand S4A is not part of sheet A (Fig. 10B); 
in the cleaved form (plakalbumin) it projects away from the 
molecule and is disordered [73], whereas in the intact 
ovalbumin it is covalently connected to strand S1C through a 
helical segment of enhanced mobility [74] (see Fig. 10A; we 
note, however, that binding loops of serpins with inhibitory 
function are very probably non-helical). In contrast to the 
modified serpin structures of al-PI*, @,-Achy* and HLEI* 
(see Table l), strands SlA, S2A and S3A in both ovalbumin 
structures are annealed with strands S5A and S6A, forming a 
five-stranded sheet A‘ (Fig. 10A, B). One obvious reason for 
Failure of strand S4A to incorporate into sheet A‘ in 
plakalbumin is steric hindrance through the bulky side chain 
of an arginine residue [73]; the equivalent Thr345 (P14) of 
cleaved a,-PI projects into the molecular centre. In all inhibi- 
tory serpins, this residue is usually threonine, and rarely valine 
or serine. 
Neither the loop structure of isolated inhibitory serpins 
nor the geometry of their complexes are yet known. Several 
lines of evidence, e. g. the susceptibility of residues P10 to P2‘ 
(residues 349 - 360 in al-PI) to proteolytic cleavage 183, 120, 
1211 and docking experiments with thrombin [122] which 
places the tightest constraints on serpin models through its 
extraordinarily narrow binding cleft [108], suggest that serpins 
might primarily interact with substrate binding sites of cog- 
nate proteinases through a particularly flat, exposed binding 
loop of canonical conformation. To confer stability to the 
formed complex without ready cleavage, the reactive site of 
this binding loop requires support through side chains 
anchored in the inhibitor core, possibly in a similar manner 
to that observed for eglin c (see Fig. 2) [58]. 
In intact and inhibitory active serpins, about four residues 
of strand S4A (after the S5A-S4A tight turn, see Fig. 10A, B) 
may be located within sheet A, with the rest looping in an 
extended conformation over the inhibitor surface to merge 
with strand S1C (Fig. 10A) [24]. That intact human cc,-pro- 
teinase inhibitor is rendered inactive upon insertion of a 
tetradecapeptide sequentially identical to Thr345 - Met358 
(i.e. its strand S4A) supports this assertion 11231 (Fig. 10A). 
In the binary peptide complex the integrated peptide obviously 
prevents the partial re-entering of strand S4A; such a re- 
entering may be required to hold the inhibitor loop segment 
in a proper conformation close to the inhibitor surface. 
Very recent results obtained for recombinant serpin inhibi- 
tor mutants with bulky residues at positions P14, P12, P10 
and P8 [124- 126, 126al would seem to confirm these ideas 
[24, 1231, according to which strand S4A must re-enter sheet 
A presumably up to its P12 residue to render a functional 
inhibitor. Latent temporarily inactive but uncleaved serpin 
forms (see [124, 1271) are observed in PAI-I and antithrombin 
111, in which this re-entering obviously occurs to a too large 
extent. 
The amino acid sequences around the active sites of 
serpins, in particular their P1 residues, match the requirements 
for canonical binding of their target proteinases. Thus, the 
geometry of the inhibitor-loop - proteinase interaction might 
to a first approximation be similar to that observed for the 
small protein inhibitors. In fact, the association kinetics of m2- 
antiplasmin and plasmin do not differ drastically from that of 
small inhibitors [128]. However, the serpin - proteinase inter- 
action might differ in many respects. Residues flanking the 
scissile peptide bond cannot be exchanged without loss of 
inhibitory potency [129] which presumably points to the im- 
portance of the spacer fit between residues of the binding 
loop and the scaffold that keeps the loop in an appropriate 
conformation. In a given serpin (e. g. a,-antiplasmin [130]) not 
only the natural scissile peptide bond, but also an adjacent 
bond might serve as the reactive site, indicating some en- 
hanced adaptability of the loop. Very recent NMR studies on 
13C-labeled serpins 11311 suggest a tetrahedral state of the PI 
carbonyl carbon in serpin -proteinase complexes. Finally, 
the kinetics of some serpin - proteinase interactions are more 
easily understood if several semistable intermediates are as- 
sumed [129, 1321.

Interaction of carboxypeptidase A with its potato inhibitor 
No atomic spatial structure of any metallo-endoproteinase 
inhibitor is known to date. The X-ray crystal structure of a 
39-amino-acid residue protein inhibitor from potatoes has 
been determined in complex with carboxypeptidase A [77]. 
The projecting carboxy terminus of the potato inhibitor (the 
four residues PI’, PI, P2, P3) is inserted into the active-site 
cleft of the enzyme and forms the primary contact region; 
the carboxy-terminal residue Gly39I is split off, but remains 
bound in the S1’ subsite, buried by the rest of the residual 
inhibitor moiety; a few additional secondary contacts confer 
stability to the complex. This complex therefore represents an 
enzyme-product intermediate in the catalytic mechanism. 
In the isolated carboxypeptidase A inhibitor determined 
by NMR [78], the projecting carboxy-terminal residue exhibits 
considerable flexibility. Upon binding to carboxypeptidase A 
it becomes ordered and presumably binds in a substrate-like 
manner, with Gly39I (which does not match the specificity 
requirements of carboxypeptidases) being slowly cleaved [77]. 
The intermolecular contacts made via its primary contact 
residues P1, P2 and P3 and the seccndary contact region [77] 
keep the truncated inhibitor in close proximity; they prevent 
rapid dissociation as well as further intrusion into the active- 
site groove. 
Pro-carboxypeptidases 
Pancreatic pro-carboxypeptidases are activated by tryptic 
cleavages of an amino-terminal segment which in pig pro- 
carboxypeptidase B (PCPB) is 95 amino acid residues long. 
This segment is folded into an open a, 8 sandwich structure 
consisting of a four-stranded antiparallel p-sheet and two 
parallel a-helices [21]. It is an autonomous folding unit [21] 
which retains its structural integrity when isolated [I471 
(Fig. 13). The pro-segment of pig pro-carboxypeptidase A 
(PCPA) remains bound to the enzyme after the first tryp- 
sinolytic cleavage, resembling an inhibitor - proteinase inter- 
action; only after a second destabilizing cleavage does it dis- 
sociate, resulting in the free, active enzyme [149]. The main 
sites of interaction are between the loop connecting the 82 
and 83 strands of the pro-segment and the general substrate 
binding area of the enzyme (including residues specifically 
involved in the fixation of substrates) and between helix x3 
and the enzyme. Several solvent molecules are integrated be- 
tween the pro-structure and the enzyme. Access to the en- 
zyme’s active site is blocked for large substrates, but the small 
inhibitor benzamidine is bound in the S1 specificity pocket of 
crystals of PCPB like an arginyl side chain of a substrate [21]. 
PCPB is enzymatically inactive while pro-carboxypeptidase A 
displays some activity against low-molecular-mass peptide and 
ester substrates [148- 1501. 
Conclusion 
All protein inhibitors of proteinases prevent access of sub- 
strates to the proteinases’ catalytic sites through steric hin- 
drance. One class of inhibitors (most serine proteinase inhibi- 
tors and the carboxypeptidase inhibitor) achieve this by bind- 
ing of a peptide segment directly to the catalytic site, in a 
substrate- or product-like manner. In the case of product-like

The second class of inhibitors comprises the cysteine pro-

to surface sites adjacent to the catalytic residues of their cog-

more often to achieve selectivity (see [146]). Pro-carboxy-
