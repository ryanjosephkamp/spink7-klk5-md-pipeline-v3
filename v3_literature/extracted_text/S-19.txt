Variants of the human pancreatic secretory trypsin inhibitor (PSTI) have been created 
during a protein design project to generate a high-affinity inhibitor with respect to some 
serine proteases other than trypsin. Two modified versions of human PST1 with high 
affinity for chymotrypsin were crystallized as a complex with chymotrypsinogen. Both 
crystallize isomorphously in space group P4,212 with lattice constants a = 844 A, c = 867 A 
and diffract to 2.3 A resolution. The structure was solved by molecular replacement. The 
final R-value after refinement with 8.0 to 2.3 A resolution data was 195% for both 
complexes after inclusion of about 50 bound water molecules. 
The overall three-dimensional structure of PST1 is similar to the structure of porcine 
PST1 in the trypsinogen complex (ITGS). Small differences in the relative orientation of the 
binding loop and the core of the inhibitors indicate flexible adaptation to the proteases. The 
chymotrypsinogen part of the complex is similar to chymotrypsin. After refolding induced 
by binding of the inhibitor the root-mean-square difference of the active site residues Al86 
to Al95 and A217 to A222 compared to chymotrypsin was 026 A. 
Keywords: X-ray structure; protease inhibitor; chymotrypsinogen; designed protein variants 
1. Introduction 
Kazal inhibitors form a well-defined group of 
competitive serine protease inhibitors that may 
show multidomain structures. These inhibitors are 
characterized by a well-preserved amino acid 
sequence containing three disulfide bridges (per 
domain) and display various specificities for serine 
proteases (Laskowski BE Kato, 1980). The pancreatic 
secretory trypsin inhibitor (PSTIP), belonging to 
this group is found in all mammalia as a transitory 
trypsin inhibitor. It is an acute-response protein in 
serum and has been seen to be massively over- 
produced in ovarian tumors. Human PST1 is a 56 
amino acid residues long protein with a strong 
inhibitory effect on trypsin, but with no effect on 
human leukocyte elastase (HLE) or chymotrypsin 
(Yamamoto et al., 1985; Laskowski et al., 1983). 
For medical purposes an effective and specific 
inhibitor for HLE could be important for the

2. Experimental Procedures 
(a) Construction of the model structure 
The 3-dimensional structure of human PST1 was 
predicted from the known conformation of porcine PST1 
in 1TGS by manual interactive substitution of the amino 
acid side-chains (73% sequence homology), followed by 
energy optimization using AMBER (Weiner & Kollmann, 
1981). A sequence alignment of the PST1 variants is 
shown in Table 1. The complex with chymotrypsin was 
modeled by superposition of residues Cysl91 to Cys201 of 
cc-chymotrypsin (Tsukada & Blow, 1985; PDB code 
4CHA) on the corresponding residues of the trypsinogen 
complex lTGS, again followed by energy minimization 
with AMBER. All calculations were performed simulating 
conditions in vacua. 
(b) Crystallization and crystallographic data collection 
Bovine chymotrypsinogen A was purchased from Serva 
(Heidelberg) and used without further purification. The 
pancreatic secretory trypsin inhibitor variants PST13 and 
PST14 were purified in fully active secreted form from 
pla.smid pMAMPF-PSTI(X) transduced Escherichia coli 
DH5a medium (Collins et al., 1989, 1990; Szardenings & 
Collins, 1990). For crystallization experiments the 
hanging drop method was employed. Complexes were 
formed immediately prior to crystallization by dissolving 
enzyme/inhibitor in an approximately 1: 2 molar ratio in 
50 m&l-Tris. HCl (pH 82). The inhibitor concentration 
was 10 mg/ml. Compact tetragonal bipyramidal crystals 
grew from 1.4 to 1.6 M-ammonium sulfate solutions 
(pH 8.2 to 8.8) within 1 to 4 weeks to a size of @2 to 
05 mm. Complexes with PST13 and PST14 proved to be 
isomorphous. The relevant crystal data are summarized in 
Table 2. All measurements were carried out using a 
Xentronics area detector mounted on a Rigaku rotating 
anode X-ray generator with monochromatic CuKa 
radiation (50 kV, 100 mA). Low intensity data (I < 20) 
gave symmetry R-values larger than 20% and were there- 
fore excluded from the data sets. The XEKGEK program 
system (Howard et al., 1987) was used for data processing. 
(c) Structure solution 
The structure was solved by molecular replacement. 
Model co-ordinates of chymotrypsinogen (Freer et al., 
1970; PDB code 1CHG) were used. These co-ordinates 
were placed in an artificial cell with 1000 A (1 A = @l nm) 
cell lengths and WO” angles in order to calculate structure 
factors for the reciprocal space rotation function 
(Rossmann & Blow, 1962). The rotation function grid was 
initially 5” for 6 to 10 A data. The molecular radius was 
set at 25 A. A search over the asymmetric unit, in Eulerian 
angles (Rao et aE., 1980) yielded a single major peak 3.3 
standard deviations above mean. Further exploration of 
this peak using a 1” raster and 4 to 8 A data gave 
0: = 13@5”, fl= 53.0”, y = 42.0” as final Eulerian angles. To 
determine the translational parameters, a reciprocal space 
translation function (Crowther & Blow, 1967) was calcu- 
lated with the correspondingly rotated chymotrypsinogen 
molecule placed at the origin of the cell. In order to 
discriminate between the enantiomorphic space groups 
P4,2,2 and P4,2,2, the translation function calculations 
were carried out in parallel for both space groups. Nearly 
all of the 7 possible Harker sections contained a single 
major peak for P4,2,2. By combination they yielded as 
translation vector O-178, 0766, OW9 (fractional co- 
ordinates for the molecular center). For P4,2,2 no con- 
sistent solution was found.

The independently refined chymotrypsinogen 
structures also show only few differences. The r.m.s.

A superposition (Fig. 3) with the structure of 
uncomplexed chymotrypsinogen (Wang et al., 1985; 
PDB code BCGA) shows differences in backbone 
conformation at A17, A35 to A38, A73 to A79, Al86 
to Al95 and A217 to A222. Excluding these regions, 
the r.m.s. difference of backbone atoms is 95 A. 
With the exception of the differences at the flexible 
loops A35 to A38 and A73 to A79 (Wang et al., 1985) 
this shows a co-operative refolding of the backbone 
induced by binding of the inhibitor. The regions 
Al86 to Al95 and A217 to A222 (Fig. 4) now form 
the sides of the specificity pocket and the oxyanion 
hole exactly as in the active enzyme and as required 
for effective binding of the inhibitor. For these 
regions the r.m.s. difference to chymotrypsin 
(Blevins & Tulinsky, 1985; PDB code 5CHA) is 
926 A for backbone atoms and 0.76 A for side-chain 
atoms. The only difference larger than 1.0 A occurs 
at Ser A218 OY with 1.6 A. The conformational 
changes in these regions induced by binding of the 
inhibitor are thus identical with the changes occur- 
ring during normal activation, which are discussed 
in detail by Wang et al. (1985). The similarity to 
chymotrypsin is shown by the r.m.s. difference of 
backbone atoms of 64 A compared to free chymo- 
trypsin BCHA and complexed chymotrypsin 
(Fujinaga et aE., 1987; PDB code ICHO). For this 
comparison only the flexible loops A36 to A39 and 
A73 to A79 were excluded together with the activa- 
tion loop A9 to A18 and the autolysis loop Al47 to 
A152, which are necessarily different. 
The autolysis loop Al42 to Al53 is brought to a 
position similar to chymotrypsin with Asn Al48 
occupying approximately the place of the side-chain 
of Asn A150 in ICHO. The cleavage site at Tyr 
Al46 is shielded by residues A218 to A220 and is 
not accessible to a protease for excision of residues 
Al47 and A148. In this position, Tyr Al46 is parti- 
cipating in the contact area to the inhibitor in a way 
similar to ICHO, but the high mean temperature 
factor of 26 A2 for this region indicates the loop as
