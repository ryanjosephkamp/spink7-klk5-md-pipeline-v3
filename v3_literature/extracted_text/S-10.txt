in SPINK5, encoding the serine pro-
tease inhibitor LEKTI, in 13 families with
Netherton syndrome (NS, MIM256500).
Most of these mutations predict premature
termination codons. These results disclose a
critical role of SPINK5 in epidermal barrier
function and immunity, and suggest a new
pathway for high serum IgE levels and
atopic manifestations.
Netherton syndrome is a severe, autoso-
mal recessive disorder characterized by
congenital ichthyosis with defective corni-
ﬁcation, a speciﬁc hair shaft defect (tri-
chorrexis invaginata or ‘bamboo hair’)
and severe atopic manifestations includ-
ing atopic dermatitis and hayfever, with
high serum IgE levels and hypere-
osinophilia1. Failure to thrive, infections
and hypernatraemic dehydration result in
high post-natal mortality.
We recently localized the NS gene locus
to D5S463–D5S2013 on chromosome 5q32
(ref. 2), in a region where the gene encod-
ing serine protease inhibitor LEKTI (for
lympho-epithelial Kazal-type related
inhibitor) has previously been mapped 3.
LEKTI was initially described in thymus
and mucous epithelia as the precursor of
two proteolytic fragments, one of which
was found to exert an anti-trypsin activity
in vitro 3. As proteolysis is critical in cell
activation and communication 4, and
because some serine protease inhibitors
have been shown to downregulate the pro-
inﬂammatory NF- kB pathway 5,6, we
assumed that LEKTI was a plausible candi-
date for NS. We therefore investigated
steady-state levels of the mRNA encoding
LEKTI in cultured epidermal keratinocytes
from a healthy control and ﬁve NS patients
from whom skin biopsies were obtained
(Fig. 1a). Northern-blot analysis showed a
3.7-kb marked hybridization signal in the
control and a marked reduction of signal in
the extracts from patients, suggesting non-
sense-mediated decay of mutated tran-
scripts, as is frequently observed in
recessive diseases7.
We therefore initiated a search for muta-
tions in the gene encoding LEKTI in NS
patients. By a combination of database
mining and PCR, we elucidated the
intron-exon layout of this gene, which we
named SPINK5 (for serine protease
inhibitor, Kazal-type 5). Mutation analysis
identiﬁed 11 different mutations in 13
families (Table 1 and Fig. 1b–f), at least 9 of
which generate premature termination
codons of translation and predict mRNA

Although it is still unknown whether
SPINK5 transcripts undergo alternative
splicing, these four splice mutations are
likely to be pathogenic because they dis-
rupt well-conserved splice elements or
generate stop codons (Fig. 1 d,e). Consis-
tent with the recessive mode of inheritance
of NS, these results predict null expression
of the mutated SPINK5 alleles through
accelerated mRNA decay or loss of func-
tion of truncated LEKTI polypeptides if
any were translated in the patients.
LEKTI is the ﬁrst serine-protease
inhibitor shown to be primarily involved
in a skin disorder or hair morphogenesis.
T ogether with the recent identiﬁcation of
cathepsin C mutations in Papillon-Lefevre
syndrome8, our study underscores the
importance of the regulation of proteoly-
sis in epithelia formation and keratinocyte
terminal differentiation. Whereas the
other disorders of keratinization feature
hyperkeratosis, a diminution or loss of the
granular and horny layers has been
described in NS patients 9, suggesting that
SPINK5 mutations cause a distinctive
defect in skin barrier function in NS.
Defective corniﬁcation may favour recur-
rent infections and inﬂammation in NS,
but the observed tissue-speciﬁcity of
LEKTI also suggests a speciﬁc role in the
thymus3. Abnormal maturation of T lym-
phocytes in NS patients may disrupt the
regulation of Th2 responsiveness to aller-
gens, which drives the acute hypersensitiv-
ity response and IgE levels10.
Genetic variants of pro-Th2 interleukins
(IL) 4 and 13 (5q31), the IL4 receptor a-
subunit (16p12) and the IL13 receptor a1-
subunit (Xq13) have been shown to be
associated with atopy, asthma and IgE lev-
els11–13. Mutations in the recombination-
activating genes (RAG) 1 or 2, both located
on chromosome 11p13, underlie Omenn
syndrome, a severe immunodeﬁciency
condition with erythroderma and a skewed
Th2 lymphocyte proﬁle 14. Our ﬁndings
indicate the existence of a novel pathway
distinct from interleukin signalling and
V(D)J recombination accounting for the
regulation of Th2 lymphocytes.
SPINK5 may regulate extracellular
matrix remodelling 4 or yet-unknown
protease-related signalling pathways, as
described for thrombin 15. Forthcoming
functional studies and in vivo targeting
of SPINK5 should unravel new molecu-
lar bases of skin barrier morphogenesis
and immunity.
